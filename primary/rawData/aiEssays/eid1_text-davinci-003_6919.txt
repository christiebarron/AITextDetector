

In recent years, censorship in libraries has become a controversial issue. Although libraries should provide information to the public in all formats, there is a need to be selective in what materials are available to the public. Certain materials, such as books, music, movies, and magazines, should be removed from the shelves if they are deemed offensive. 

The primary objective of censorship in libraries is to protect the minds of young people from being exposed to certain materials that may be deemed inappropriate or offensive. The studies have shown that young people are more likely to be exposed to and influence by such materials, and this could have an adverse effect on their development. Consequently, there is an urgent need to restrict access to such materials in libraries. 

Censorship in libraries does not mean that books, music, movies, and magazines that are deemed controversial or offensive are banned. It rather means that they would be hidden from the undiscerning eyes of the public, or they would be displayed in a way that will make them less accessible to young people. Libraries should put rules in place that would ensure that adults have access to all available materials, but such materials should be kept away from children and young people. 

In addition to protecting youths, censorship in libraries also serves to protect the moral and social values of our society. By removing certain materials that are considered offensive or inappropriate, libraries are helping to promote and uphold the values established by our society.
