Censorship of library materials is a highly controversial subject. On one hand, some argue that some books, music, movies, and magazines should be removed from the library shelves if they are found to be offensive to some people. On the other hand, it has been argued that such censorship is a violation of the Freedom of Speech and curbs creativity and expression. Both sides present valid points and should be discussed in detail.

The Freedom of Speech is a fundamental right that we all enjoy. Therefore, censorship of material such as books, music, movies, and magazines is a direct violation of this right. Telling someone that they cannot read, play, or watch something they want to does not bode well with the principles we hold dear in our society. It stifles creativity and expression by denying people the chance to open their minds to new ideas and arguments. 

On the other hand, it could be argued that libraries need to censor certain books, music, movies, and magazines if they are found to be offensive to some people. No one should be subject to violence, discrimination, or other forms of hatred that is found in some materials. It is important to protect the public from offensive materials and provide a safe and welcoming atmosphere in our libraries.
