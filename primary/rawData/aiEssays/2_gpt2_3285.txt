I believe that censorship of materials in libraries and other public institutions is a key element in maintaining fair and effective distribution of copyrighted material. Although copyright in the media is not intended to discriminate in or against particular individuals, it has been established that copyright in such materials may sometimes be protected by the First Amendment. The U.S. Supreme Court, in In re J.M. Miller, 566 U.S. 490, 579 -980 (1999), observed that 'copyright in copyright' or others may be protected when the media is 'intended for some purpose to promote, direct and disseminate the public good or to inform others of a social or political cause not intended for the purpose of public goodcould not be protected by the First Amendment. Although the Supreme Court recognized that 'no free press is the first refuge of freedom from the undue influence of the press.' In Stegman v. United States, 379 F.3d 1135, 1145 (5th Cir.1995), that Court held that the First Amendment protects an author not only against the undue influence and censorship of his or her work, it also protects him or her against undue personal influence. That Court concluded that the First Amendment does not provide protection from the distortion or manipulation of his or her 'content without great and sustained public concern.'
