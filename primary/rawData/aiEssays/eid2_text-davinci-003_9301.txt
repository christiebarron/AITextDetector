

In an increasingly diverse world, libraries have become a gathering place for people of all backgrounds, beliefs, and values. The global community is interconnected with an ever-growing sharing of information, and libraries are a key source of that. However, libraries have quite often been the target of censorship and restriction of resources. Libraries should not be subjected to censorship, for it is damaging to our rights, strips freedoms that are fundamental to a library’s core mission, and is ultimately ineffective. 

First, censorship of library materials should be kept out of libraries because it is a violation of the first amendment of the United States Constitution that states, “Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances.” Censorship of library materials interferes with free speech and freedom of expression, which is a fundamental right recognized in the United States Constitution. Library resources are integral to the exercise and engagement of free speech, and access should not be restricted.

Further, censorship impairs the mission of libraries, which is to provide general access to an abundance of resources and empower people to do research, exercise their free speech and expression, and engage in conversations.
