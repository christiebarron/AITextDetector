

A cursory glance at the bookshelves of any library is evidence of just how far our society has advanced in terms of knowledge and culture. Libraries form an integral part of this advancement, allowing readers access to a vast array of materials to explore and further their learning. However, this progress is threatened when censorship is introduced. It is not uncommon for certain materials, such as books, music, movies, and magazines, to be found offensive by some readers. Given the potential infringement upon a person’s right to freely access and explore such materials, should these materials be removed from the shelves if they are found to be offensive? 

The answer to this question is an unequivocal no. Censorship, although seemingly well-intentioned, not only limits access to materials which may prove immersive and educational to readers, but it is also an assumption that some readers, whether by reason of age or maturity, are either unable or unwise enough to make their own reasoned decisions about what they read or view. However, this assumption does not take into account the individual’s intellect, nor his or her capability to discern what is right and wrong for them without any form of censorship. 

It is also important to consider the implications of censorship in libraries. Censoring books inherently prohibits them access to information, thoughts, and opinions which may otherwise enable them to make informed decisions about their beliefs and the world.
