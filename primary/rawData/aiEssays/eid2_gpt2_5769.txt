I believe that censorship of materials in libraries are an inappropriate response to those who would threaten an individual's privacy," the email reads.

"This is not about any specific censorship of free speech or freedom."

The letter is part of a broader list of messages released by the National Security Agency to civil liberties advocates over the growing number of documents that it says the NSA is intercepting from the digital world. Several have already seen the highest legal consequences. The NSA said in a statement on Monday it will not be making any new public requests — the most recent one was in 2008 — to review the documents or publicize them.

Fellow American actor James Bond revealed on Twitter the NSA had asked his company and Twitter to help put online an online archive of "sensitive data," while he told CNN that the agency had tried to prevent "an embarrassing act."
