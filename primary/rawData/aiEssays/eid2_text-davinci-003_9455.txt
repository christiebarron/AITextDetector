

When it comes to censorship in libraries, opinions are divided. Some believe that certain materials, such as books, music, movies, and magazines should be removed from the shelves if they are found to be offensive. They argue that such material is not appropriate for public consumption and can have a negative impact on the community. However, there are those who argue that censoring library material is counter-productive and can lead to a censorship of ideas.

Proponents of censorship often cite the need to protect children from books, art, and music that are deemed offensive. They suggest that libraries should be required to review the content of material before it can be made available to public library patrons. This would prevent children from being exposed to content that could be detrimental to their mental and emotional wellbeing.

On the other hand, advocates of free access to all material argue that censoring library material violates the United States Constitution, which guarantees freedom of speech. They suggest that people should have the right to access any material that is intended for adults, and that censorship hampers our right to access information. While objectionable material should be given a rating and age restriction, proponents of free access argue that it should not be completely removed from the library.
