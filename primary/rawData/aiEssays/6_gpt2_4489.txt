The builders of the Empire State Building faced several obstacles in allowing dirigibles to dock.

The United Nations's Security Council imposed an economic embargo on China by banning their use in buildings built or used by Chinese citizens. China rejected the prohibition as the sole source of economic aid to the State Administration of Construction. Instead, officials claimed that China had an economic interest in supporting its citizens, especially those working in the U.S.-China Association of Commerce and Industry and for building U.S.-owned U.S.-owned factories.

After the U.S. Supreme Court affirmed the Commerce Clause by a 17-2 vote, the United Nations and China's government sent legal challenges to the ban. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed by a 4-3 vote. The appeal was heard by Judge Harold Williams of the U.S. 4th Circuit and Judge William Paulson of the U.S. 9th Circuit. The Supreme Court said in their concurring opinion: "The decision of the Seventh Circuit does not allow for the importation of large quantities of large quantities of materials of all conceivable dimensions, even materials of all kinds, if a commercial purpose justifies the importation of them as part of an economic activity."
