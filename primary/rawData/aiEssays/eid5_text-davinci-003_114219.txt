

Narciso Rodriguez’s memoir, From Home: The Blueprints of Our Lives, serves as a powerful reminder of the importance of connection within one’s culture and heritage. Rodriguez’s narrative style creates an emotive atmosphere in which readers feel a strong sense of nostalgia for the past. His stories evoke a feeling of longing for home, family, and more innocent times.

The memoir is dripping with nostalgia and evocative descriptions of Rodriguez’s family home in Hoboken, New Jersey— the colorful people, the Sunday lunch picnics, and the strong sense of community surrounding him all create an atmosphere of warmth and familiarity. Rodriguez is also sure to include his memories from visits to his extended family in Cuba— his grandmothers’ wisdom and the feeling of safety within the home fit comfortably into his narrative. 

Roderigez’s writing also manages to capture the bittersweet feeling of nostalgia— his reflections on the past and on the struggles his family experienced in moving to the United States add a layer of complexity to his writing. In this way, he is able to capture both the joys and pains of the immigrant experience with remarkable depth.
