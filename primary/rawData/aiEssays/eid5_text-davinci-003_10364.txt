

Narciso Rodriguez’s memoir, From Home: The Blueprints of Our Lives, is an exploration of his journey as a creative immigrant. Through his expressive writing style, Rodriguez creates a sense of nostalgia and melancholy that resonates with the reader.

From the start of the memoir, Rodriguez conveys a strong sense of longing and nostalgia for his family’s Mexican heritage. His vivid descriptions of childhood memories spent in Mexico bring the reader into those moments and evoke a strong sense of feeling. Through the lens of Rodriguez’s childhood perspective, the reader can sense a longing for simpler times and an innocent familiarity with the culture. 

Beyond nostalgia, Rodriguez’s memoir also creates a somber mood. The narratives of his parents’ immigration journeys, and the struggles of his family in their new home, build a heavy backdrop of sadness and alienation. Throughout the memoir, Rodriguez speaks of fear, monotony, and hardship. He shows a struggle between his own culture, his family’s, and his newfound American identity. 

When Rodriguez reflects on his eventual success, the tender, melancholy mood is juxtaposed with the triumph of Rodriguez’s American Dream. In this way, Rodriguez creates a mood that is bittersweet. His sensitivity towards his own identity allows the reader to appreciate the toll of such profound success.
